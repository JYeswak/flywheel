# Regional gym — lead intake rebuilt as written work a machine can run

I help SMB owners buy their time back.

This is one of the operating fixes that proves how. The owner runs a regional
gym. The marketing person quit on a Friday. The Monday email cadence still
fired. The leads still came in. The follow-up did not.

This is the shape of the work I do. The case study lives in two parts: what
the operating route looked like before, and what I built so the next time
someone walks out, the work stays.

## Before — what the day looked like

The owner walked the floor at 6am, opened the day at the front desk, took
two new-lead calls before coffee, and ended Saturday rebuilding the Monday
report from the four places that held pieces of it. The marketing person
held the rest in their head. When they left, the head left with them.

Concretely, the operating route was:

1. **A lead lands** — sometimes via a web form, sometimes a referral, sometimes a walk-in at the front desk.
2. **Someone has to notice** — and they have to know which channel.
3. **The first reply** — varies in tone, sometimes same-day, sometimes Monday.
4. **The tour booking** — the owner's calendar, by text, manually negotiated.
5. **The follow-up** — depends on who remembers.
6. **The re-engagement** — depends on the same person remembering, six weeks later.

Five of those six steps lived in one head. When that head left, the route
ended at step two.

## The Peel — discovery week

I sat with the owner for the Peel (Week 1). The work was not the tooling.
The work was naming the route. We walked the path from "customer asked" to
"the right person did the next thing" and wrote down where the path stopped
short. Five places, all at human-memory boundaries.

The Peel produced a written map of those five stop points. The owner read
it back to me and added two more I had missed (the desk-walk-in channel,
the trial-class-no-show class). The map was now real because the operator
could read it without paraphrasing.

## The Press — the build

The Press (Weeks 2–8) rebuilt the intake path as written work a machine
could run, without changing the route the owner already trusted.

What I built:

- **Capture** — one shared inbox that received leads from every channel. Web form, referrals, front-desk capture (a small tablet form), trial-class sign-ups. Each row carried the channel, the timestamp, and the operator who fielded it (so the owner could see by name later, never as a leaderboard).
- **Routing** — the inbox routed by lead type to the right operator. Front-desk leads went to the floor lead; web leads went to the trial-class coordinator; referrals went to the owner so she kept her hand in. No leads went nowhere.
- **Reply** — a written first-reply template per lead type, sent by the routing rule within 15 minutes. Operators could edit before send, but the default was never silence.
- **Tour booking** — a single link in every reply, mapped to the correct calendar by routing rule. The owner stopped reading "what's a good time for you" emails on her phone at the desk.
- **Re-engagement** — a written sequence for the trial-no-show class, with a stop point. After three checkpoints, the system handed the lead back to the owner with a one-line summary and a recommendation, then stopped firing.

The first re-engagement sequence ran in week 3. By week 8, the owner read
the system output Monday morning instead of rebuilding it.

## The Pour — launch and what compounds

The Pour (launch + ongoing) is the part that earns its name. The system is
operating; I tune it. The owner keeps her judgment; I keep the route
documented and inspectable.

What you can check, on the system, today:

- Every captured lead has a row, a channel, a timestamp, an owner, a status. The owner reads the row, not a recollection.
- Every reply has a sent timestamp and the template used (or the operator edit). When a reply was late, the row shows why; when a reply was wrong, the next iteration of the template is one git commit away.
- Every booked tour has a link in the lead row, and the no-show class has the re-engagement timeline attached. The 6-week-later question is answered by the row.

The owner runs a smaller front-desk shift. The marketing-person-shaped hole
in the operation got filled by written work, not by another hire. When the
next hire eventually came, the new person inherited a documented operating
route instead of a head full of context.
